AT&T LONG LINES BUILDING

Date 1974

TypeUtility

Location 33 Thomas Street, New York City, NY 10013

The AT&T Long Lines Building, located at 33 Thomas Street in Lower Manhattan, is one of New York City's most enigmatic skyscrapers. Standing at 550 feet (170 meters), this Brutalist structure has no windows and was designed to withstand nuclear blasts, making it one of the most fortified buildings in the world.

Architectural Design and Purpose

Designed by architect John Carl Warnecke, the building was completed between 1969 and 1974 as a major hub for long-distance telephone communications. It was constructed to house AT&T’s switching equipment, which required a controlled environment, free from natural light, fluctuating temperatures, and external threats. The thick concrete walls and lack of windows help to maintain stable conditions for telecommunications infrastructure inside.

The facade consists of flame-treated Swedish granite panels, giving it a monolithic and imposing presence. The structure includes 29 floors, but due to the immense height of each floor—averaging 18 feet—it is equivalent in size to a typical 40-story building.

Inside The Building

Unlike conventional skyscrapers filled with office spaces, the Long Lines Building is primarily a cavernous, industrial facility. The majority of the interior consists of vast, open floors filled with telephone switching equipment, data servers, and power systems. The floors are reinforced to support heavy machinery, and its self-sufficient design includes its own water and fuel supplies to maintain operations for weeks in case of an emergency.

Access to the interior is highly restricted, with tight security measures in place. Employees who have worked inside describe the environment as eerily quiet, apart from the constant hum of machinery. The air is filtered, and emergency generators ensure the building remains operational even in the event of citywide power failures.

The NSA's Alleged Involvement

In 2013, classified documents leaked by former NSA contractor Edward Snowden suggested that the AT&T Long Lines Building may be more than just a telephone exchange. According to reports by The Intercept, the building allegedly houses a top-secret surveillance program known as TITANPOINTE, which is believed to be operated by the National Security Agency (NSA) in partnership with AT&T.

The building's role in global intelligence efforts is suspected to involve the interception of phone calls, internet traffic, and other forms of communication. Documents revealed that AT&T, through the Long Lines Building, may have facilitated the NSA in gathering data on foreign and domestic communications, acting as a hub for mass surveillance programs. While neither AT&T nor the NSA have confirmed these allegations, the secrecy surrounding the building has only fueled speculation.

Cultural Impact and Urban Legends

The Long Lines Building’s fortress-like design has made it a subject of urban legends, conspiracy theories, and pop culture references. It has been featured in books, films, and television shows, often depicted as a secure government facility or a dystopian stronghold. Some theorists claim that deep underground tunnels connect it to other parts of the city, though no evidence has confirmed this.
